ISSUE #2

The 15-Hour Work Week

Keynes predicted it. Technology enabled it. Why are we still grinding? Explore the evolution of time, productivity, and the radical idea of reclaiming our lives.

Evolution of the Clock

Chronicles 1890-2024
Era 1

The Industrial Grind

70-hour weeks were the norm. Time was mechanized, strictly monitored by foremen, and tied directly to physical output in the factories.

Era 2

The 40-Hour Compromise

Ford normalized it. The unions fought for it. The 9-to-5 became the golden standard of the 20th century middle-class existence.

Era 3

The Automated Future

As AI and robotics decouple labor from output, the 15-hour week isn't a luxury—it's an economic necessity to distribute wealth and time.

The False Promise of Infinite Growth

In 1930, the economist John Maynard Keynes predicted that within a century, technological advancement would be so profound that we would only need to work 15 hours a week. He believed our greatest challenge would be figuring out what to do with all our leisure time.

We hit the productivity targets he imagined decades ago. Yet, the work week stalled at 40 hours. Instead of trading productivity gains for free time, we traded them for consumption. The system demanded infinite growth, inventing 'bullshit jobs' to keep the machine churning.

It is time to audit our existence. The 15-hour work week is not about doing less; it is about living more.
